Portfolio Analysis Reference

In order to manage your business cybersecurity risks, you will typically create strategic program initiatives such as Zero Trust Architecture, Detection and Response Program, Supply Chain Risk Management and so on. We refer to these Strategic Initiatives as Portfolios.

A Portfolio is effectively a collection of controls that mitigate various business risks. We want to develop a model that systematically decomposes the effectiveness of your strategic initiatives in managing risks through a Monte Carlo simulation of the effectiveness of controls in each of the portfolios in combating threats.

The more risk buy-down that we are able to achieve with a strategic initiative, relative to the cost associated, the more return on investment we achieve with that initiative.

Portfolio Efficacy Simulation

We estimate the expected efficacy of our portfolio of controls by conducting a Monte Carlo simulation of the control’s effect on a simulated stream of threats thrown at the control.

The sketch of the simulation process is as follows:

  1. For each business risk, simulate all the MITRE ATT&CK techniques associated with the business risk by generating a stream of random threat events following a Poisson threat event arrival process
  2. For each strategic initiative, stream the threats to the portfolio of controls and measure the failure or success of the control in treating each simulated threat.
  3. Under the mitigation success scenario, record the Control Effect, (see description), which we later use as statistics to measure the overall effectiveness of the strategic initiative.

Threat Arrival using a Poisson Process

We are interested in simulating the effectiveness of controls in mitigating threats. There are many factors that affect a control’s efficacy, which we discuss in the section on Control Efficacy. However, we need to model the arrival of threats over time that the control must interact with. We choose a Poisson distribution as it is an appropriate distribution for modelling discrete stochastic processes, such as arrival rates. The arrival rate of the Poisson process is λ\lambdaλ. For example, if the yearly average number of times that the threat materialises is 1000, then the parameter λ=1000\lambda=1000λ=1000 is provided to the Poisson distribution, and samples drawn from the Poisson distribution for each day of the year to simulate the occurrence of threats over time.

Reasoning about control effectiveness decay

The Poisson based approach to simulation of threat arrivals over time is important because it allows us to model a natural decay aspect of controls that happens in practice over time if the control is not well managed. For example, an antivirus or a next generation firewall that does not keep its detection signatures up-to-date. We can model this by taking into account when last a control was validates as still working as intended, for example, using breach simulation software. See more details about this under Control Efficacy.

Monte Carlo Simulation of Threat Mitigation by Controls

The streams of threat events generated by the Poisson random process are fed into our controls mitigation simulator to understand how the control will treat the threat. The probability that the threat will be mitigated by a control is dependent on several aspects of the control posture, which is discussed in detail under Control Efficacy.

Calculating a weighted sum of Control Effects

We have associated with each control an Effect aligned1 to the NIST Cyber Security Framework (NIST CSF for short). The Effect of a control is what the control does to a threat, which could be to Identify, Detect, Protect it, or to Respond to it or Recover from it. For example, a Network Intrusion Detection System, has a Detect effect, whereas a Network Intrusion Prevention system has a Protect effect on threats.

The control effects have different impact on risk reduction, which we capture by assigning weights to the effects. During the Monte Carlo simulation, for each portfolio of controls we calculate the risk reduction potential of the portfolio by calculating expected risk reduction across the portfolio controls by counting and calculating a weighted some of the effects of the controls within the portfolio when the controls treat the simulated threats. The table below describes the weights assigned to each control effect.

Respond15%Takes action after detection. Responding can limit impact, but some damage might have occurred.

Calculating the RoI of a Portfolio

The Portfolio Risk Reduction Weight, WPW_PWP​ gives us a measure of the risk dampening effect of a portfolio of controls, and is used directly in calculating the return on investment of the portfolio.

This is done by first calculating the Expected Risk Removal, RPR_PRP​, of portfolio PPP.
