Risk Analysis Reference

This section provides a technical description of how risk modelling works in Risk Insight.

FAIR at the Core

Risk Insight analyses are Monte-Carlo simulations, and we use the FAIR framework for the risk analysis. This is quite standard for credible information security risk management.

At a high level, FAIR defines risk as

Where LEF is the Loss Event Frequency, that is how frequently (typically annualised) a loss event might occur, and LM is the Probable Loss Magnitude, which characterises the financial cost each time the organisation experiences a loss event.

At a glance

For the LEF and LM estimates that we provide in the Business Risk templates, we use Poisson Log Normal and Log Normal distributions respectively to model and generate the estimates. We however expose the data in the user interface as equivalent PERT parameters and collate your organisation-specific estimates also using PERT parameters of minimum, most likely and maximum estimates. We however implement a translation between PERT and the relevant underlying distribution.

The conversion details are provided below.

Generating LEF

Estimating LEF for your organisation could be daunting due to lack of data, but using dataset from past incidents could inform a credible prior to your analyses.

The IRIS Risk Retina research conducted by the Cyentia Institute, shows that the arrival rate of material cybersecurity events could be modelled with high fidelity by a Poisson Log-normal process, which tightly fits actual Cyber Loss Data gathered over a 10-year period by Advisen.

A key takeaway from the IRIS research is that incident frequency varies across sectors, and within a sector, it differs by the organisation’s size measured by revenue. These in turn impact the appropriate parameters (mean: μ\muμ and standard deviation: σ\sigmaσ) for the Poisson log-normal generative process that describes the incident frequencies.

As described above, we have codified these mappings across the various business sectors (as defined by NAICS) and sizes, which informs the selection of a tailored generative distribution for the LEF in the Business Risk Template section of Rapid Risk Assessments when you select your organisation’s business sector and revenue in Risk Insight.

User interface considerations for LEF

Since the loss event frequency and loss magnitude estimates that you provide for your organisation as a subject matter expert involves uncertainty, we use PERT parameters to represent and collate this information. We also argue that it is more natural and intuitive to provide your company-specific loss event frequency estimate in terms of the minimum, most likey, and maximum values, of a PERT distribution; as opposed to asking for the mean and standard deviation parameters of a Poisson log-normal to model your company’s LEF. The same argument goes for the specification of your company’s Loss Magnitude.

Thus, in order to simplify LEF estimates gathering and intuitive reasoning about the LEF, you provide the data in the form of minimum, most likely and maximum, with optional “Confidence” factor (Gamma or γ\gammaγ) for PRO users, which captures the shape, i.e. the spread of your confidence across those trifecta of estimates. Internally, we map from PERT to log-normal distributions, abstracting you away from the difficulty of reasoning about log-normal means and standard deviations to keep the user interface more intuitive and natural, and importantly to capture your degree of uncertainty in the estimates.

See the details of the mapping between PERT and Log-normal here

Generating LM

The LM (Loss Magnitude) can be modelled by a log-normal distribution. The parameters to the log-normal distribution are the mean μ\muμ and standard deviation σ\sigmaσ. These vary according to the sector the organisation belongs to.

User interface consideration for LM

To capture uncertainty in expert estimates, and also because it is more natural and intuitive, we collate your company-specific loss estimate in terms of the minimum, most likey, and maximum values, which can be used to generate a PERT distribution; as opposed to asking for the mean and standard deviation to be used in a log normal model of your company’s probable loss magnitude. This is however not a barrier to modelling loss magnitude using a log normal process, because it is fairly straightforward to map log-normal distribution to a PERT one, and vice versa. We provide the details of the mapping between these two processes as used in Risk Insight below.

Note to the purist

Purists among you may already be protesting that a PERT distribution mapped this way may not perfectly fit the Log-normal distribution or vice versa. We however want to suggest not sweat this too much as to lose sight of our objective, which is to support credible decision making. For this goal, the process is sufficient to enable directionally correct decisions, such as for comparing returns on cybersecurity program investment or for assessing the relative impact of risk outcomes or the relative efficacy of various controls in mitigating cybersecurity risks. What we are after is accuracy, not precision, and you are way better off than folks doing categorical risk assessments with High, Medium, and Low.

However, if one must validate the degree of fit between these distributions after conversion, techniques such as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests can be used to validate how well the mappings fit each other. We leave that as an exercise.

Categorical Risk Breakdown

We call out specific cybersecurity risk categories such as Ransomware and Extortion, Business Email Compromise, Business Disruption etc. to enable you to break down specific risks and to analyse them so that you can understand what your biggest risk drivers are.

To enable you to do this, we have mapped the relative loss event frequencies and loss magnitudes from historical breach data to your business sector and size, which is provided as guide values in the Business Risk Template illustrated above. Consider these values as guides backed by historical data providing you informed priors for your estimates, but we expect that you will tailor the values to appropriate estimates for your organisation’s risk exposure.
