Evidence-driven Belief Update

Anchoring Our Simulation with Objective Data and Telemetry

When the Posteriori Hits the Fan!

My prior belief that I’d ace this statistics exam was high, but after observing the actual test questions, my posterior has collapsed to a delta function centred on a failing grade.

We began our technical discussion by noting two sources of unpredictability in our analyses of risk, namely inherent variability, due to external risk factors such as the threat landscape, threat actor capabilities and our objective attack surface, versus our own uncertainty, due to knowledge limitation.

When we provide subject-matter expert (SME) estimates, there is always some element of uncertainty associated. We can however reduce the unpredictability induced by our own uncertainty through various methods, such as by running testing and validation of the control environment. This section is focused on how we can take a data-driven approach to managing uncertainty in SME estimates.

Reducing Uncertainty with Evidence and Data

A reasonable question to ask is, how can we use data and evidence from our control environment to reduce uncertainty associated with SME estimates?

Risk Insight facilitates a systematic and rational grounding of our risk analyses in reality by allowing SME estimates to be updated with evidence from test data and telemetry from the control environment. This enables a data-driven and scientifically-rigorous methodology for updating our belief about SME estimates in a logically-consistent way.

Why update belief?

Many of the inputs to Risk Insight simulation, such as intrinsic control efficacy, control efficacy decay, control coverage, control response time etc., start with SME estimates. This estimate is what the SME believes to be a true representation of reality of their control environment. However, as we conduct various tests and validation of the environment, for example, through penetration testing, red team exercises, breach simulations and also as we gather metrics such as successful attacks and near misses; we should be able to update the SME estimate to more closely align with the data and evidence, ensuring that analysis results are grounded in reality.

This has the effect that our simulations and risk estimates are taking into account real-world posture, thus reducing the effect of expert uncertainties and biases.

That is precisely what we achieve through the Bayesian belief update methodology. The approach is a well-known scientific technique called Bayesian Belief Update Method or Bayesian Inference, which we describe next.

In order to determine the parameters of the conjugate prior Beta(α,β)\text{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)Beta(α,β), we provide options for the SME to provide a hint about their confidence in the estimate and we set that to the standard deviation σ\sigmaσ, which is a measure of spread around the SME’s estimate. We set the estimate to be the mean μ=E\mu = Eμ=E of the prior belief Beta(α,β)\text{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)Beta(α,β).
