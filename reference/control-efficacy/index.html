Control Effectiveness Reference

This section provides the details of how we calculate a control’s efficacy.

Calculating the Threat Mitigation Potential of a Control

At the core of our Monte Carlo simulations of the effectiveness of control Portfolios is how we model the effectiveness of individual controls. Several factors go into this calculation

We use the following parameters to simulate how effective a control would be in mitigating threats

  1. Mitigation effectiveness (Efficacy): this is a measure, in percentage, of how well the controls does what it is meant to do. For example, what is the probability that an Antivirus will block the execution of malware, or for a firewall, the probability that it will block malicious traffic?
  2. Deployment coverage (Coverage): this is the extent, in percentage, that the control is applied/deployed to the relevant assets.
  3. Continuous Validation: how often is the control tested that it is working as intended? We model this with an exponential decay function. The idea is that the longer we left a control without testing or validation, the lower our confidence that the control is still performing as intended. So, we introduce a natural decline, Δ′\Delta'Δ′, in its efficacy, to capture our uncertainty about its effectiveness.

These factors lead us to a concept of a control’s Threat Mitigation Potential, which is the probability that a control will mitigate a threat that it encounters. We define the Threat Mitigation Potential below.

Before we define the concept of control efficacy decay over time modulo its validation cadence, Δ′(t,v)\Delta'(t,v)Δ′(t,v), let us first describe the concept of control efficacy decay over time when the control is not continuously validated as effective.

Modelling non-monitored Control Efficacy Decay over time.

Controls do fail for various reasons, for example, due to misconfiguration, or due to missing detection signature updates, or due to a clash with another control, and the list goes on. In order to maintain assurance that the control will work as intended when needed, we must continuously test the control to verify that it meets its intended threat mitigation objectives. We model the effect of the passage of time ttt (in days) on the confidence that we have in a control’s effectiveness with an exponential decay function Δ(t)\Delta(t)Δ(t) defined below.

Δ(t)=e−ktControl Efficacy Decay over time, t\begin{aligned} \Delta(t) & = e^{-kt} & \gray{\footnotesize \text{Control Efficacy Decay over time, $t$}} \\ \end{aligned}Δ(t)​=e−kt​Control Efficacy Decay over time, t​

The efficacy decay rate parameter, kkk, is an indication of our appetite for risk around the efficacy of the control, because it controls how quickly our confidence in the control’s efficacy drops with time. The higher kkk is, the more quickly that confidence drops.

Continuous Validation and Efficacy Decay

Whenever we test a control as functioning as expected, this validation effectively resets the decay back to the initial value. This is modelled as follows, suppose vvv is the number of days between testing of the control. The resultant efficacy decay, Δ′\Delta'Δ′, for the control is now defined as

Δ′(t,v)=e−k×(t mod v)Control Efficacy Decay over time, with validation\begin{aligned} \Delta'(t, v) & = e^{-k \times (t \ \text{mod} \ v)} & \gray{\footnotesize \text{Control Efficacy Decay over time, with validation}} \\\\ \end{aligned}Δ′(t,v)​=e−k×(t mod v)Control Efficacy Decay over time, with validation

Simulating Threat Mitigation

Now that we have an efficacy measure, we can perform a Monte Carlo simulation to study the effect of a control on threats.

Γ(r,t,v)={mitigated,if r<Efficacy×Δ′(t,v)× Coveragenot mitigated,otherwise \begin{equation} \Gamma(r,t,v) = \begin{cases} \text{\small mitigated}, & \text{if $r < \text{Efficacy} \times \Delta'(t,v) \times $ Coverage} \\\\ \text{\small not mitigated}, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \nonumber \end{equation}Γ(r,t,v)=⎩⎨⎧​mitigated,not mitigated,​if r<Efficacy×Δ′(t,v)× Coverageotherwise​​

Γ(r,t,v)\Gamma(r,t,v)Γ(r,t,v) represents a single experiment, where rrr is a random number picked from a uniform distribution, and ttt is the simulated day when the threat event occurs, and vvv is the validation cadence of the control in question. If the mitigation potential (Efficacy ×Δ′(t,v)×\times \Delta'(t,v) \times×Δ′(t,v)× Coverage) of the control exceeds the random number rrr, then the control mitigates the threat and we count the number of times that happens, collating the effects of the control, whether it is Protect, Detect, Respond and so on; versus when the control fails to mitigate the threat. This is what goes into the calculation of WPW_PWP​ as described in the Portfolio Efficacy Simulation section.
